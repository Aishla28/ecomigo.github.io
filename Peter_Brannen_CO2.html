How climate works

‘Listen to the science. Before it’s too late’

Carbon Dioxide: The Invisible Player in Earth's Story

Carbon dioxide (CO2) plays a critical role in Earth's ecosystem, but disrupting its natural balance can have dangerous consequences. This text explores the importance of CO2 and the potential threats posed by human activity.

The Lifeblood of Plants

Sustainability is not a spectrum with varying degrees of achievement. It's a fundamental change in how societies function. We can't achieve true sustainability by making minor adjustments to our current way of life. A complete transformation is necessary, from our energy sources and consumption patterns to our industrial practices and agricultural systems. This large-scale shift requires commitment and innovation, but it's the only way to ensure a healthy planet for future generations.

Past Disruptions and Extinction Events

he geologic record provides evidence of past mass extinction events that coincided with significant disruptions in the carbon cycle. For instance, the Permian extinction, one of the worst extinction events in Earth's history, is believed to have been caused by a massive volcanic eruption that released enormous amounts of CO2 into the atmosphere. These events highlight the potential dangers of a rapid imbalance in CO2 levels.

Human Activity: A New Threat

The burning of fossil fuels like coal, oil, and natural gas releases vast quantities of CO2 into the atmosphere at an unprecedented rate. This disrupts the natural carbon cycle and has the potential to tip the balance, leading to a rise in global temperatures and potentially catastrophic consequences for the planet.

Earth's Temperature Blanket

CO2 plays a crucial role in regulating Earth's temperature. It acts like a blanket in the atmosphere, trapping heat from the sun. This trapped heat keeps our planet warm enough for life to thrive. Without this natural greenhouse effect, Earth's average temperature would be much colder, making it a frozen wasteland. However, there's a delicate balance. Too much CO2 in the atmosphere can trap excess heat, leading to global warming and significant changes in climate patterns.

The Carbon Cycle: A Balancing Act

atural processes are constantly adding and removing CO2 from the atmosphere, maintaining a delicate equilibrium. Volcanic eruptions, for example, release CO2 into the air. On the other hand, the ocean absorbs some CO2, and living organisms like plants and plankton remove it through photosynthesis. Through these natural processes, the amount of CO2 in the atmosphere has been kept relatively stable for millions of years, creating a suitable environment for life to flourish.
