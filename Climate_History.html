How climate works

‘Listen to the science. Before it’s too late’

The Discovery of Climate Change - Michael Oppenheimer

This section discusses the history of climate change science, highlighting the challenges faced in raising awareness and taking action. Here's a look at the milestones and struggles along the way.

Early Warnings Ignored

The concept of human-caused climate change isn't a recent invention. As far back as 1896, a Swedish scientist named Svante Arrhenius theorized that human activities like burning fossil fuels could trap heat from the sun and warm the planet. Unfortunately, his groundbreaking ideas were largely ignored by the scientific community at the time.

The decades that followed saw a gradual shift. By the 1970s, as more research was conducted and evidence accumulated, a stronger consensus began to emerge within the scientific community. The link between human activities and global warming became increasingly undeniable.

The Hurdles of Communication and Politics

Communicating the urgency of the climate crisis and convincing policymakers to take action proved to be a significant challenge. Scientists faced an uphill battle in getting their message across. Powerful fossil fuel industries often exerted pressure on politicians, and some even resorted to spreading misinformation to downplay the severity of the issue, creating confusion and hindering progress.


The international community attempted to address the global climate crisis through landmark agreements like the UN Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) in 1992 and the Kyoto Protocol in 1997. These agreements set goals for emission reductions, but they had limitations. For instance, they lacked strong enforcement mechanisms to hold countries accountable, and they exempted developing countries from mandatory reductions, limiting their overall effectiveness. Additionally, major emitters like the United States withdrew from the Kyoto Protocol, further jeopardizing international efforts.

Europe Takes the Lead

Some European countries, particularly Germany under Angela Merkel's leadership, emerged as champions for climate action. A combination of strong political will and public pressure in these countries pushed for stricter environmental regulations and a shift towards renewable energy sources.


The 2015 Paris Agreement marked a significant step forward in the fight against climate change. This international agreement, unlike its predecessors, included participation from a wider range of countries and aimed to limit global warming to well below 2 degrees Celsius, ideally 1.5 degrees Celsius, compared to pre-industrial levels. However, rising emissions from developing countries with rapidly growing economies, like China and India, threaten the agreement's effectiveness.

A Reason for Optimism

Despite the challenges, there are reasons for hope. Advancements in clean energy technologies offer promising solutions for reducing our reliance on fossil fuels. Public awareness about the climate crisis is growing, and younger generations are demanding action from their leaders. These factors, combined with strong political will and international cooperation, can pave the way for a more sustainable future for our planet.
