Why Didn't We Act on Climate Change? Unveiling the Obstacles

This section discusses the reasons behind the delay in taking action on climate change, despite the scientific evidence.

The Industry's Shadow of Knowledge

The troubling role of the fossil fuel industry, particularly major oil companies like ExxonMobil. ExxonMobil's own internal documents reveal a disturbing truth: they were aware of the potential climate risks associated with their products as early as the 1970s. However, instead of acting responsibly and working towards solutions, they chose to downplay these risks to the public. This lack of transparency and willingness to prioritize profits over environmental well-being is a major reason for the delay in addressing climate change.

The fossil fuel industry, along with allied industries like coal and car manufacturers, did not simply remain silent. They actively employed various tactics to create confusion about climate science. These tactics bear an unsettling resemblance to those used by the tobacco industry to downplay the health risks of smoking. Some of their strategies included cherry-picking data to fit their narrative, funding biased research that supported their agenda, promoting fringe scientists with dissenting views to create a false impression of scientific uncertainty, and attacking the credibility of legitimate climate scientists who presented inconvenient truths. They also deflected blame by focusing on individual actions to reduce carbon footprints, rather than advocating for systemic changes within the energy sector. These disinformation campaigns successfully sowed seeds of doubt in the public mind, hindering public support for climate action.

Free-Market Ideology: Aligning Interests for Stasis

Some free-market think tanks promoted economic policies that prioritized deregulation and limited government intervention. These ideologies aligned perfectly with the interests of the fossil fuel industry, which sought to avoid restrictions on their activities and maximize profits. This alignment between industry and economic thought leaders further entrenched the status quo and made it difficult to consider alternative energy sources or regulations that might address climate change.

A Broken Economic System: Prioritizing Profit Over the Planet

The current economic system, with its focus on short-term profits and shareholder value, is part of the problem. This system incentivizes continued reliance on fossil fuels, even when the long-term consequences for the environment are dire. The pursuit of profit often trumps considerations of sustainability, hindering the transition to cleaner energy sources.

This economic model is causing broader environmental problems beyond just climate change. Our current system may be encouraging practices that are harmful to the environment in various ways, from deforestation and pollution to the depletion of natural resources. Addressing climate change requires a broader transformation of this system to prioritize long-term environmental health.

Psychological Resistance to Change: Protecting Power and the Status Quo

People in positions of power, particularly those with close ties to the fossil fuel industry, may be resistant to acknowledging the industry's role in downplaying climate science or the flaws within the economic system. Change can be disruptive, and powerful interests may fight to protect the status quo, even when it comes at the expense of the environment. Overcoming this resistance is crucial for taking decisive action on climate change.


By understanding the root causes of inaction, such as the fossil fuel industry's disinformation campaigns and the limitations of our current economic system, we can begin to develop solutions and move towards a more sustainable future. Once we recognize the obstacles, we can work to dismantle them and pave the way for a more sustainable future for our planet.
