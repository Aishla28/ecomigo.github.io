A Sobering Look at Humanity's Impact on the Planet (Civilization and Extinction)

This page delves into the sobering reality of humanity's impact on the planet's biodiversity. It paints a clear picture of how our actions, throughout history, have significantly altered ecosystems and driven many species to extinction. Here's a closer look.

The Rise of Modern Humans and Global Expansion

Our story begins around 200,000 years ago with the emergence of Homo sapiens in Africa. Over countless generations, our ancestors embarked on a remarkable journey, migrating and eventually populating the entire globe.

The Disappearance of Megafauna

concerning pattern emerges as we examine the arrival of humans in new territories. Large animals, often referred to as megafauna, like mammoths and giant sloths, started disappearing within a relatively short geological timeframe wherever humans set foot. Overhunting by our ancestors is believed to be the primary cause of this mass extinction event.

European Colonization and a Spike in Extinction

The arrival of Europeans in new territories marked a significant increase in the rate of extinction. Hunting by colonists for food and sport, coupled with the introduction of new predators like cats, dramatically impacted native animal populations. The dodo bird, a flightless bird from Mauritius, is a tragic example of an extinction directly linked to European colonization.

The 20th Century: A Turning Point for Our Planet

The 20th century witnessed an exponential rise in human population growth, consumption of resources, and exploitation of the natural world. This dramatic increase in our impact significantly intensified the negative consequences for the environment.

To feed a growing population, vast swathes of forests were clear-cut and converted to agricultural land. This habitat destruction had a devastating impact on biodiversity hotspots like the Amazon rainforest, home to a staggering number of unique species.

Climate Change on the Move

Climate change is forcing animals to adapt to rapidly changing environments. Species that cannot adapt quickly enough face extinction. Unfortunately, human-made barriers like walls and roads can further restrict their ability to migrate to more suitable habitats, adding another layer of pressure to their survival.

Rising water temperatures disrupt the delicate symbiotic relationship between corals and algae. These algae provide corals with nutrients and give them their vibrant colors. When the water becomes too warm, the algae are expelled, leading to coral bleaching and widespread reef decline. Bleached corals are more susceptible to disease and death.

The Bramble Cay melomys, a small rodent native to a tiny island off the coast of Australia, is likely the first documented extinction directly caused by rising sea levels, a consequence of global warming.
